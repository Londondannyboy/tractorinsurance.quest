## How Your Contribution is Calculated

### Step 1: Identify Your Pensionable Pay

**Pensionable pay** typically includes:

- Basic salary
- Some allowances (depending on contract)
- Unsocial hours payments (in most cases)
- Overtime (for some members)

**Not usually included**:

- Non-pensionable allowances
- Expenses
- One-off bonuses

<Callout type="info" title="Check Your Payslip">
Your payslip should show your "pensionable pay" figure. This is what's used to calculate your contribution, not your gross pay.
</Callout>

### Step 2: Find Your Tier

Look at the table above to find which tier your annual pensionable pay falls into.

### Step 3: Calculate Your Contribution

Multiply your pensionable pay by your tier's percentage rate.

## Tax Relief on Contributions

You receive **tax relief** on your NHS Pension contributions. This means your pension costs less than the headline rate.

### How Tax Relief Works

NHS Pension contributions are taken from your pay **before** income tax is calculated. This gives automatic tax relief.

## Part-Time Workers

If you work **part-time**, your contribution rate is based on your **actual earnings**, not the full-time equivalent.

### Example

<Callout type="example" title="Part-Time Example">
**Emma** works 3 days per week. Her full-time equivalent salary would be Â£35,000, but she actually earns Â£21,000.

Her contribution tier is based on **Â£21,000** (Tier 3 at 6.1%), not Â£35,000.
</Callout>

This means part-time workers often pay a lower percentage than their full-time colleagues on the same grade.

## Multiple NHS Jobs

If you have more than one NHS job:

- Each job has its own contribution rate
- Rates are based on each job's pensionable pay separately
- All jobs count towards your total pension

<Callout type="info" title="Multiple Jobs">
If your combined NHS earnings would put you in a higher tier, that doesn't affect individual job contributions. Each is calculated separately.
</Callout>

## Contribution Holidays

You can temporarily stop contributing (opt out) and rejoin later. However, this is rarely advisable:

### Why You'd Lose Out

- No employer contributions during opt-out
- No pension growth for that period
- No death or ill-health cover

<Callout type="warning" title="Think Carefully">
Opting out loses you the 23.7% employer contribution - effectively a 23.7% pay cut in pension terms. Most financial advisers recommend staying in.
</Callout>

## Salary Sacrifice Arrangements

Some NHS employers offer **salary sacrifice** arrangements for benefits like:

- Cycle to work schemes
- Childcare vouchers
- Additional pension contributions

These **reduce your pensionable pay**, which could lower your pension contributions but also your pension benefits.
