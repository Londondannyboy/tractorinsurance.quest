## Phased Retirement

Teachers can take a **phased retirement** - drawing some pension while continuing to work:

### How It Works

1. Reduce your working hours by at least 20%
2. Draw part of your pension (with early reductions if applicable)
3. Continue building pension on remaining work
4. Take full retirement when ready

### Benefits

- Gradual transition to retirement
- Income from both pension and salary
- Continue accruing pension on remaining service

<Callout type="info" title="Phased Retirement Rules">
You must have reached age 55 (57 from 2028) and be employed as a teacher. Your employer must agree to the reduced hours.
</Callout>

## Actuarially Reduced Benefits (ARB)

If you leave teaching but want to access your pension early, you can request **Actuarially Reduced Benefits**:

- Available from age 55 (57 from 2028)
- Pension reduced to reflect early payment
- You don't need to have left teaching completely

### Staggered Retirement Strategy

You could:

1. Draw pre-2007 benefits at 60 (no reduction)
2. Draw 2007-2015 benefits at 65 (no reduction)
3. Draw 2015 benefits at SPA (no reduction)

This avoids early retirement reductions but means waiting longer for some benefits.

### The Break-Even Point

The break-even point is typically around **age 82-85**:

- Before that: Early retirement gives more total pension
- After that: Waiting would have given more total pension

<Callout type="tip" title="Think Long-Term">
Average life expectancy at 65 is about 20 years. If healthy, waiting for full pension often pays off.
</Callout>

## Ill Health Retirement

If you're too ill to teach, you may qualify for **ill health retirement**:

- **Total incapacity**: Enhanced pension if you can never work
- **Partial incapacity**: Pension based on service to date

This is different from voluntary early retirement - no early payment reductions apply.
