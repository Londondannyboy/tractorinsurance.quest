Good news: you don't have to have worked to receive the **State Pension**. You can build up qualifying years through National Insurance credits, which are free and given automatically in many situations.

## How to Qualify Without Working

The State Pension is based on your National Insurance (NI) record. While most people build this through working and paying NI contributions, you can also qualify through **National Insurance credits**.

## Grandparent Childcare Credits

If you're a grandparent (or other family member) who looks after a child under 12 so their parent can work, you may be able to claim **Specified Adult Childcare credits**.

### How It Works

1. The child's parent must be working
2. The parent transfers their NI credit to you
3. You get a qualifying year towards your State Pension

## What If I Don't Have Enough Years?

If you don't have 10 qualifying years, you may be able to:

### 1. Pay Voluntary Contributions

You can pay Class 3 voluntary National Insurance (Â£17.45/week per year) to buy additional qualifying years.

### 2. Claim Credits You've Missed

Check if you're entitled to credits you haven't claimed:
- Carer's Credit for past caring
- Credits for fostering
- Credits for jury service

### 3. Continue Building Credits

If you're under State Pension age, keep building credits through:
- Claiming benefits you're entitled to
- Registering as a carer
- Claiming Child Benefit

## Your Partner's State Pension

Your State Pension is individual - you can't share or inherit your partner's State Pension entitlement while they're alive.

However, if your partner dies, you may be able to inherit some of their State Pension, depending on:
- When they reached State Pension age
- Their National Insurance record
- Whether they deferred their pension
