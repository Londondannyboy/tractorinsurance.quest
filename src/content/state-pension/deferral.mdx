**State Pension deferral** means choosing to delay when you start claiming your State Pension. In return, you receive a higher amount when you do claim. But is it worth it?

## How Deferral Works

When you defer your State Pension:

- You don't receive any payments during the deferral period
- Your State Pension increases by **1% for every 9 weeks** you defer
- This works out to approximately **5.8% per year**
- The increase is for life once you start claiming

## The Break-Even Point

When you defer, you "lose" pension payments during the deferral period. You need to live long enough after claiming for the higher amount to make up for this.

## When Deferral Makes Sense

Deferring your State Pension may be a good idea if:

### 1. You're Still Working

If you're earning a good income past State Pension age:
- You don't need the State Pension income
- The State Pension is taxable - it may push you into a higher tax bracket
- Deferring reduces your tax bill now

### 2. You Have Other Income

If you have sufficient other income (workplace pension, investments, savings):
- You don't need the State Pension immediately
- Deferral gives you a guaranteed "return" on your money

### 3. You're in Good Health

If you have good life expectancy:
- You're more likely to exceed the break-even point
- The extra income will benefit you for longer

### 4. You Want to Leave More to Family

If you're married/in a civil partnership:
- Your surviving spouse may inherit some of your deferred pension
- The higher amount continues after your death (subject to rules)

## When Deferral Doesn't Make Sense

Deferring probably isn't right if:

### 1. You Need the Money

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't Defer If You Need Income">
If you're struggling financially, claim your State Pension. No increase is worth financial hardship.
</Callout>

### 2. You Have Health Concerns

If your life expectancy is below average, you may not reach the break-even point.

### 3. You'd Use Savings Instead

Don't use up other savings just to defer State Pension. Your savings may be needed for emergencies or care costs.

### 4. You'd Work Longer Just to Defer

If you're only working to avoid claiming State Pension, this usually doesn't make financial sense.

## How to Defer

Deferral is automatic - simply **don't claim** your State Pension when you reach State Pension age.

You'll receive a letter about claiming around 2 months before your State Pension age. If you want to defer:
- Don't respond to the letter
- Your State Pension will automatically be deferred

### How to Start Claiming Later

When you're ready to claim:
- Apply online, by phone (0800 731 7898), or by post
- You can backdate your claim up to 12 months
- Your first payment will include the higher rate

## Partial Deferral

You cannot take a partial State Pension - it's all or nothing. Either you:
- Claim the full amount at State Pension age, or
- Defer the entire amount

<Callout type="info" title="No Flexibility">
Unlike some private pensions, you can't take some State Pension now and defer the rest. It's a binary choice.
</Callout>

## Tax Implications

Your State Pension is taxable income. Deferral affects your tax in two ways:

### During Deferral

- You don't receive State Pension payments
- Your taxable income is lower
- You may pay less tax on other income

### After Claiming

- Your State Pension is higher
- Your taxable income increases
- You may pay more tax overall

For most people, the lifetime tax impact is roughly neutral. But if you're a higher-rate taxpayer now and expect to be basic-rate in retirement, deferral could save tax.
